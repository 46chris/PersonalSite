23BuJo

Organizing a hectic schedule

Timeline - March 29 - June 11, 2021
Roles - Frontend Engineer, Designer

Overview

23andMe's Bullet Journal was created in a group of nine student developers over the course of ten weeks. It was created as a solution for organizing college student's hectic schedules.

Problem

Rarely in life is anybody able to have the luxury of having a single thing to focus and worry about. This holds especially true for college students who have to balance their academic, extracurricular, and career responsibilities while maintaining some semblance of a social life.

Handling such a hectic schedule could lead to missed deadlines and hurt one's mental health. The bottom line is, college students need a way to handle their hectic schedule to healthily tackle their academic and career-oriented goals.

Bullet Journal Research

Our first order of action was researching the Bullet Journal domain. After reading through blog posts, lavish product descriptions from companies shilling their online BuJo's, and insights from a handfule of BuJo users (COVID restrictions), we identified our philosophy and statement of purpose for our app

Philosophy: A BuJo provides a single place for keeping track of events, tasks, and notes. Rapid logging, the language of the BuJo, makes the content easy to read. A color scheme and media content makes the space more personal.

Statement of Purpose: Our project aims to create a digital version of the typically analog bullet journal while maintaining the same level of function and personality.

Plan the Minimum Hope for More

As a group of 9 inexperienced developers, we thought it best to nail down the essential featuers of our app and then move onto peripheral features if we had time. Based on what we learned in our preliminary research, we decided that the four features below were essential to the BuJo experience.

Mood tracker
Transferable bullets
Month, Week, Day Views
3 Bullet Types

The Harsh Reality of Feasibility

I was a part of a group of 4 designers in charge of designing the UI for the BuJo. Our initial designs (below) contained feautres such as customization of the background, drawing, and complex user statistics. Though we thought these features made our UI 'look cool' they were wholly unrealistic and much of our low-fi designs had to be scrapped as a result. On the bright side though, the general look of our pages (side navbar and daily page) remained largely unchanged up through the final prototype.

Although a lot of work was for nothing, this design iteration was a good learning experience. I was reminded that user experience and feasability must always be balanced. I think that my group was a bit over eager to create an impressive design which made us overlook the fact that implementing many of these features required far more work than we had time for.

Ask for Second Opinions

In our final iteration of our BuJo design, we decided to present our prototype to our team and professor. Though everybody on the team generally enjoyed the look and feel of the prototype, our professor told us that the intentions of some pages were unclear and the app as a whole looked far too 'Christmasy'. Since our professor was the sole stakeholder in this practicum course, we immediately worked to address some of his concerns. First, we overhauled the theme to a less-diverse color palette as our professor denoted the previous one seemed a bit 'cluttered'. Then, we increased the contrast on UI elements to make interactable elements clearer for the user.

Admittedly, the design overhaul didn't really 'overhaul' the design as much as it could've. This was mainly because coding had to start as soon as possible and we believed that the design could be reworked as we coded frontend elements. This ended up being impossible given the time crunch and was an important reminder that designs should be tested outside of the team echo chamber.

Final Product

Below are two of the main user flows demoed on our web-app.

Drag and Drop

Transferable bullets are a key functionality to a Bullet Journal. The demo above shows how different bullets can be moved from different days within the Weekly view. There are 8 days displayed at a time so that a bullet can be transferred from one week to the next.

Day view bullet creation

The daily view contains an EntryCreator component that allows the user to create 3 different types of bullets (task, event, and note). The day scroller component at the top allows users to move between days. Bullets are also draggable within this view to allow for the user to sort the bullets however they deem fit within a day.

Reflection

As a whole, I was happy with how the project turned out. This was the first time I've had to implement a server and backend to an app and the database and authentication services works with minimal bugs even today. Even though a lot of the frontend logic could be optimized and organized much better, it culminates into a very usable UI with minimal bugs or errors.

If I were to have had a couple more weeks with the project, I'd probably spend most of it redesigning the UI. I think that the blue background seems a bit overwhelming and the top bar with the logo and profile seems a bit unpolished. Furthermore, the date switcher at the top of the daily view looks a bit rough and the collapsible side bar's animation and placement is a bit awkward.

Nevertheless, this project was an indispensible coding learning experience that really developed my understanding of vanilla Javascript and server-side coding. It was a great introduction to Agile methodologies which has been invaluable in my internships at SmartTwigs and United Health Group.
