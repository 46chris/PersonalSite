Timeline

September 25, 2021 - October 10, 2021

Defining the Product

During the first meeting with the client, I aimed to define the key features of his website. As he is still a student, he emphasized that the site must contain a contact form and make his socials easily accessible. Aside from that, we added an about page as that is the standard-fare for these portfolio sites.

Mood / Flair

The client was unsure on what kind of atmosphere and flair he wanted on his site. To give him some ideas, I compiled others' portfolio sites and presented it to him to see if there was any layout, color scheme, or design choices he liked. Unfortunately, this didn't change much and he suggested I make the website as simple and plain as possible. I stupidly listened and did just that.

Prototyping

After looking at the simplest of the reference sites, I went to Figma to prototype his website. I created three layouts all adhering to a monochromatic color scheme to keep in line with his wishes for a simple site and ensure that his artwork was the focal point of the site.

So I set up a meeting and presented these layouts to the client. He said he enjoyed the simple look of his site and began to pick and choose elements he liked from each one. He said he liked the layout of the art from options 1 and 3 (leftmost and rightmost layouts), the about section of option 1 (leftmost), the navbar of options 1 and 2 (leftmost and middle), and the color scheme used in option 1 (leftmost). Using his feedback, I began creating the final prototype.

Final Prototype

Ultimately, I chose to go for a single page website to adhere to the client's wishes of a simple site. Other than that, I just took out the pieces he enjoyed from the previous prototypes and made this one

First Deployment

Because this was just going to be a static webpage with little changes in the future, I chose to deploy the site on github pages (also it's free and I'm broke).

Learning

My biggest issue with this website was overengineering. I don't know if it was because I was just coming off of finishing developing an app for a class or I was just extra paranoid but I was so obsessed with optimizing the speed of the website. I tested all the different ways to instantiate the images on the DOM in order to figure out which was the fastest when in reality, it really didn't make a difference.

I also devoted far too much time trying to make the website as future-proof as possible. This entailed trying to find all the "backendless" (without backend cause github pages doesn't support it) ways to make adding in future art pieces as painless as possible. Without going into detail, this was also a waste of time considering that simply adding in the image to the repo and then adding in an img tag to the html took < 1 minute.

Second Deployment

The client ended up being happy with the first deployment. I was not. The page was far too dull and I thought, was doing a disservice to his artwork. So I offered to program a new site for him at no extra cost. If he didn't like the new one, I told him he could simply keep the old one.

Redesign

It isn't good practice, but seeing as though I had a good idea of what I wanted his new site to look like, I just went straight to coding it up.

My biggest gripe with the first site was the ugly navbar at the top so I decided to change it for a side navigation bar with a "logo" that I designed myself utilizing some of his older art pieces as the background. I also lightened the color scheme because the black seemed too dreary. Finally, I opted for a multi-page site because it kept everything organized and opened up the possibility to give the site a bit more personality.

Feel free to give his site a visit here!

Final Thoughts

My biggest takeaways with this project was to always take what the client wants with a grain of salt and to not overengineer things. Despite recognizing that what the client wanted would look sub-par, I created the site anyways because I thought it best not to question what the client wants. After redesigning the site and seeing the client much happier with a layout that I thought would look better, I learned that my opinion as a developer holds weight and shouldn't always be ignored in favor of the client's. As for over-engineering, I think this stems from my naturally anxious personality. I'm sure that as a I learn more about the ins and outs of web development, this will get better.

As a whole though, I'm happy with how my first client's site turned out and can't wait to start on more.
