Onwards

For facilitating group decisions

Timeline - August 2 - September 4, 2021
Roles - Designer, UI / UX Researcher

Overview

"Onwards" is an app design created in a group of 6 students over the course of 5 weeks. It's an activity-finding and decision-facilitating travel-based app.

Problem

Groups of travelers planning out their itinerary need a way to facilitate group discussion because it can be hard to voice individual opinions, especially when some are not as social as their peers.

Background

Imagine it's your last day vacationing with your friends in Hawaii. You've always wanted to visit the Byodo-In temple but the outspoken leader of the group has swayed the group to laze on the beach. You think some people would prefer to visit Byodo-In but are unsure. This is where Onwards comes in. With anonymous voting on a centralized list of potential destinations, Onwards easily surveys each individual's preferences to help your group make the optimal decision on your next outing.

Brainstorming Pain Points

With only five weeks to fully flesh out our app, my group went straight into identifying any pain points related to traveling. We quickly identified two major ones that all 6 of us identified with

  • Finding attractions the align with our interests
  • Creating an itinerary everybody is happy with when traveling in a group

Current Solutions

After identifying the two pain points, we looked for apps built to solve these issues to get a feel for what was already on the market. The four that we thought were most similar to what we were envisioning for Onwards were Citysocializer, Planmesh, Peatix, and Mesh.

☝ Citysocializer, Planmesh, Peatix, Mesh

Some apps were catered specifically to simplifying planning when planning in groups, whereas others were meant for solely suggesting activities to do. None had both features integrated onto the same platform.

Identifying the MVP

From here, we moved onto identifying the core features of our app. We began by listing every feature we thought a group-making-travel app should have. After group voting and discussion, we decided our MVP should have these features:

  • A friend list and group list (each trip will be organized by "group")
  • Group creation
  • A chat room and voting room in each group
  • A voting system where each group member leaves some sort of rating on each activity in a group's prospective activity list.
  • Ability to search for activities and places to eat and then add into a group's voting list
  • Ability to leave ratings and reviews on activities and places to eat

Competitor Analysis

A groupmate and I were in charge of the competitor analysis. After identifying core features of similar apps and the general UX, we figured that Onwards would be most successful if it required minimal on-boarding. Similar apps such as Planmesh and Eezy had lengthy onboarding processes that in our opinion, would divert users away from ever using their services because of the high upfront cost. We also wanted Onwards to focus primarily on facilitating group decision making while maintaining the search capabilities of apps such as Tripadvisor and Google.

Identifying our user Groups

Every group member was reponsible for creating three personas and a corresponding story board. The personas I created consisted of a couple, group of friends, and an individual. These three personas were created based on the decision-facilitating and activity-finding capabilities of Onwards. The group and couple were meant to represent the most common use case for Onwards where groups can find and decide upon an activity through Onward's main flow. The individual on the other hand represents a less-common use case and mainly portrays the activity-finding capabilities of the app.

Learning

Overall, I think it was beneficial to focus on the nature/size of the group using our app in our storyboards. Keeping the nature of different group dynamics at the forefront of our user-based development ended up shaping much of our UI/UX choices during the mockup and prototyping phase.

The actual drawings in the storyboards however could've incorporated more of personas and focused less on the screens within the app. There are too many frames with just the app and the lack of guiding signs (such as arrows) makes the storyboard visually confusing. I think this was the case because we were too eager to start developing the app and turned the storyboard into a UI brainstorm rather than a use case brainstorm.

Learning

Creating the mockup was a messy process because we failed to elaborate past our initial brainstorm of our MVP and just haphazardly jumped onto Figma together. Everybody had their own idea of what the app should look like and contain which made our first drafts extremely incoherent.

After hours of essentially going nowhere, I suggested we take a step back and identify the specific pages the app should have. From there, we chose an icon package to keep our icons consistent and tackled each page as a group to ensure that everything was coherent.

In the future, I would definitely run a group meeting identifying the specific pages we need to mockup before actually creating it. Furthermore, I think it would help the cohesiveness of the mockup to either tackle each page as a group or create some rudimentary style guide to keep everything consistent.

Final Prototype

Below are three of the main user flows (group creation, searching / adding activities, and voting) demoed on our figma app.

👈 Group Creation

Since groups are integral to the app, group creation and addition were clearly indicated on the home screen. Users can add a title, photo, location, and attendees to a group which will have its own chatroom and voting area for chosen activities.

👈 Search and Add

Each attendee can search for prospective activities using our tag-based filters and sorting features. Each activity has its own page with ratings and reviews that are left by our user base and basic info (like location and what it is).

👈 Voting

Each activity that each group member adds to their group will be available to be voted on in the group's 'activities' tab. Each activity can get a desirability rating of 1-5 and the activities will be sorted from highest rated to lowest rated. Users can also view what other group members rated each activity in the comprehensive vote tab.

Conclusions

Altogether, I was happy with the work my group was able to accomplish in a five week summer quarter that was conducted completely online.

If we had more time, I would've done a lot more user testing. The little user testing that we were able to do was organized by the professor and was limited to the other groups within our class. Despite this, we gained valuable information into how our app should look and function and the feedback largely carried the design changes we made from the mockup to the final prototype.

As for myself, the biggest takeaway from this experience was to not underestimate the importance of getting to know your teammates. In previous projects, my relationships with teammates was limited to whatever we talked about for work and in meetings. During my time on Onwards, I got to know my teammates and relate to them on things outside of the app. I felt that this made us more honest with each other during feedback sessions and made the meetings much more enjoyable to be a part of.
